How Much Does Custom Software Cost for a Small Business? (2026 Breakdown)

Custom software for a small business usually costs $8k–$60k in 2026. Here's the real price range, five cost drivers, and a worked example.

TL;DR

Custom software for a small business typically costs between $8,000 and $60,000 in 2026, depending on scope. Price is driven by five specific factors: number of integrations, number of user roles, data migration, how branchy the workflows are, and the builder's hourly rate. A simple internal tool from a mid-tier developer usually lands around $12,000–$20,000 to build, plus roughly 15–20% of that per year for maintenance.

Key takeaways

  • A light custom internal tool costs $8,000–$15,000 in 2026; a mid-scope tool costs $15,000–$35,000; a heavier multi-role system costs $35,000–$60,000+.
  • Hourly rates in 2026 range from $25–$49 (offshore agency) to $100–$200 (US/EU agency), with freelancers at $30–$80 and a fully loaded in-house developer around $63/hour (US BLS data).
  • Ongoing costs include hosting ($50–$400/month), third-party API fees, and maintenance at roughly 15–20% of the build cost per year.
  • Custom software beats per-seat SaaS on total cost once you have enough users or enough workflow oddities that the SaaS stops fitting.
  • Getting an accurate quote requires four inputs: a workflow document, an integration list, a list of user roles, and rough data volumes.

Custom software is a purpose-built application designed for one specific business's workflows, instead of a generic SaaS product that thousands of companies share.

What "custom software" actually means for a small business

Custom software, for a small business, is usually a small internal tool built to run one specific workflow better than any off-the-shelf app can. Think a job-dispatch board, a client-onboarding portal, an inventory reconciler, or a quoting calculator wired into your accounting system. It is not an "enterprise platform." It is a focused tool that does one job for your team, well.

The typical price range in 2026

Most small business custom software projects in 2026 fall into three tiers. Here is what each one usually buys you.

  • Light ($8,000–$15,000): A single-purpose internal tool. One or two integrations, one or two user roles, no historical data migration. Example: a form-to-database tool that replaces a messy spreadsheet.
  • Mid ($15,000–$35,000): Two to four integrations, multiple user roles, some conditional workflows, light reporting. Example: a job-scheduling and crew-dispatch app.
  • Heavy ($35,000–$60,000+): Five or more integrations, role-based permissions, historical data migration, dashboards, and edge-case handling. Example: a full client portal with billing, document sharing, and staff assignment.

Above $60,000, you are usually paying for either a bigger user base, regulated data (healthcare, finance), or a customer-facing product rather than an internal tool.

The 5 things that actually drive the price

The price of custom software is driven mainly by five specific factors, not by how "custom" it feels.

  1. Number of integrations. Each system it talks to (QuickBooks, Stripe, Google Calendar, a warehouse API) adds real hours. Integrations are usually the biggest single cost driver.
  2. Number of user roles. A tool with one user type is fast. A tool with admin, manager, and field-worker views is roughly two to three times more work.
  3. Data volume and history migration. Moving five years of messy spreadsheet data into a clean database can add 20–60 hours by itself.
  4. Workflow branching. "If the job is over $5,000, route to the owner for approval" — each rule like this is a branch. More branches, more testing, more hours.
  5. Builder rate and type. The same tool built by a $40/hour freelancer versus a $180/hour US agency can differ by 4–5x on paper (though quality and speed vary a lot).

Hourly rates by builder type in 2026

Hourly rates in 2026 vary widely by builder type, and the range matters more than the average.

  • Freelancers: $30–$80/hour. Best for small, tightly scoped projects.
  • Offshore agencies: $25–$49/hour (GoodFirms, 2024 data still holding). Cheaper on paper, more overhead in communication and rework.
  • US/EU agencies: $100–$200/hour (Clutch). Higher rate, typically fewer hours because of experience.
  • In-house developer: roughly $63/hour fully loaded in the US, based on BLS median wage plus benefits and overhead.

Ongoing costs people forget

Every custom tool has three recurring costs beyond the build.

  • Hosting: $50–$400/month for a small business tool on AWS, Google Cloud, or a managed platform.
  • Third-party API fees: SMS (Twilio), payments (Stripe), maps (Google Maps), email (Postmark) — usage-based, usually $10–$150/month for a small team.
  • Maintenance: budget 15–20% of the original build cost per year for bug fixes, dependency updates, and small feature additions.

When custom beats SaaS on cost

Custom software beats SaaS on cost when your per-seat SaaS bill, multiplied by users and years, exceeds the build plus ongoing costs. The math is simple: divide your annual SaaS spend into the build cost to get your break-even year. Most small businesses paying $300+/month across two or three overlapping tools hit break-even inside three years.

Worked example: Bright Path Landscaping

Bright Path Landscaping has 12 employees and needs a job-scheduling and crew-dispatch tool. It pulls jobs from QuickBooks, sends route info to crews via Twilio SMS, and uses Google Maps for optimization. Two user roles (dispatcher, crew lead). No historical data migration.

  • Build: 140 hours × $95/hour = $13,300
  • Hosting + Twilio: ~$85/month
  • Maintenance: ~$2,400/year (about 18% of build)
  • Replaced: two SaaS tools costing $340/month combined = $4,080/year

Break-even vs SaaS: roughly month 26. After that, Bright Path saves about $1,700/year, and owns the tool.

How to get an accurate quote

To get an accurate quote, hand the developer four things: a one-page workflow document, a list of every system the tool must talk to, a list of user roles and what each can do, and rough data volumes (records, users, monthly transactions). Without these, quotes vary 5–10x because each developer is guessing at scope. With them, quotes usually land within 20% of each other.

FAQ

What's the minimum realistic budget for custom software in 2026?

Around $8,000 for a light, single-purpose tool built by a mid-tier developer. Below that, you are usually looking at no-code platforms like Airtable or Retool rather than true custom software.

Why do quotes for the same project vary by 5–10x?

Because scope is usually vague and hourly rates vary from $25 to $200. A tight brief (workflow doc, integrations, roles, data volumes) shrinks that spread dramatically.

Is it cheaper to hire a freelancer or an agency?

A freelancer is cheaper on paper for small projects. An agency is usually better value for anything with multiple integrations or roles, because agencies have testers, project managers, and someone to call when the freelancer goes on vacation.

How much should I budget for ongoing maintenance?

Budget 15–20% of the original build cost per year. A $20,000 tool needs roughly $3,000–$4,000/year for updates, dependency patches, and small fixes.

When does custom software beat a monthly SaaS subscription on cost?

Custom wins when your combined SaaS spend for a workflow exceeds roughly $300/month, or when the SaaS is forcing you to change how you work. Break-even is usually inside three years.

Can AI coding tools make this cheaper in 2026?

Yes, modestly. AI-assisted development is cutting build hours 15–30% for straightforward tools, which shows up as lower quotes from developers who use it well. It does not reduce integration or testing time much.
